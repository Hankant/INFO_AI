# Defense Brief

## One-sentence version

This project does not simply ask whether people trust AI. It asks whether seeing a structured AI answer first changes how much evidence people think is enough, whether they feel the knowledge is truly theirs, and how strongly they update when counterevidence appears later.

## Recommended defense storyline

1. Start with the real-world shift
   Generative AI changes the sequence from "evidence first, judgment later" to "answer first, verification later."

2. Then explain the theoretical gap
   Prior work discusses algorithmic trust, algorithm aversion, and automation bias, but it does not fully explain how AI changes perceived information sufficiency, epistemic ownership, and the diagnosticity of later counterevidence.

3. Then present the core framework
   The revised version works best as a three-part calibration framework:
   - sufficiency calibration
   - ownership calibration
   - diagnosticity calibration

4. Then explain why the revision is stronger
   The original design had a major problem: AI search and traditional search have inherently different objective search costs.
   The revised same-summary source-label design narrows the question to this:
   when summary content and marginal follow-up search cost are held constant, does the AI label itself change later search and updating?

5. End with the research significance
   The project does not try to prove that AI is always harmful.
   It shows that AI can reshape metacognitive calibration, which matters for information design and AI governance in high-risk environments.

## Three points worth emphasizing in the defense

- The revised version is not weaker; it is cleaner in identification.
- The contribution is not only that users may search less, but that the project explains why they may feel there is no need to search more.
- The study can speak to both AI efficiency gains and AI-induced distortions, rather than treating AI as only a problem.
