# Experiment Design Review

## 1. What clearly improved in the revised draft

- The revised draft no longer compares "AI search" and "traditional search" as total packages, so it avoids the biggest identification problem: objectively different search costs.
- The same-summary source-label design is a strong move because it keeps initial content constant and lets the post-summary label do the causal work.
- The revised version introduces `epistemic ownership`, which makes the project more distinctive than a pure search-depth study.
- The yoked logic from the original draft is still valuable because it can separate "saw less" from "weighted less."

## 2. How I would explain the revision in the defense

- The original draft asked a broad but noisy question: does AI change evidence sampling and belief updating?
- The revised draft asks a narrower but cleaner question: when summary content and marginal search cost are held fixed, does an AI label change perceived sufficiency, perceived value of additional evidence, ownership, and later updating?
- This is a methodological improvement, not a retreat. It makes the causal claim more credible while preserving the larger theoretical ambition.

## 3. What still needs strengthening

### A. Pretest the label manipulation

- Make sure `AI-labeled`, `expert-labeled`, and `unlabeled` versions differ in perceived source identity without unintentionally changing perceived quality, competence, or formality.
- If the AI label also makes the summary seem more technical or more recent, the treatment is no longer just "AI source label."

### B. Add objective ownership measures

- Self-reported ownership is useful, but it should not stand alone.
- Add at least one behavioral or performance measure:
  - recall of supporting reasons,
  - ability to reconstruct the evidence logic,
  - transfer of the judgment rule to a nearby case.

### C. Use more than one scenario

- The default-risk scenario is analytically convenient, but the committee may worry about task-specificity.
- Add at least one second vignette, such as medical risk or policy risk, even if only as a robustness study.

### D. Pre-register moderators

- Prior AI trust
- Numeracy or probabilistic reasoning ability
- Domain familiarity
- Preference for self-search versus delegated search

These moderators help answer whether the effect is stable or concentrated in particular user types.

### E. Keep a clean normative benchmark

- The strongest part of the original design was the computable Bayesian benchmark.
- Preserve it wherever possible:
  - fixed likelihood ratios,
  - fixed shock strength,
  - explicit log-odds outcome,
  - clearly defined optimal stopping benchmark when using search cost.

### F. Add robustness outcome metrics

- `Brier score`
- `log score`
- direction of update
- willingness to pay for one more signal

These will make the empirical story less dependent on a single measure.

## 4. My overall judgment

The revised design is directionally better and more defensible. Its strongest contribution is that it turns a broad "AI changes search" claim into a more precise metacognitive-calibration study. Before final defense or pre-registration, the most important next step is to strengthen manipulation validity and ownership measurement.
